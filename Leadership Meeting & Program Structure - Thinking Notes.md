# Leadership Meeting & Program Structure — Thinking Notes

*Date: April 17, 2026*
*A working document — captures a thinking-through conversation about whether/how to restructure the FASD leadership meeting and the underlying program structure questions it surfaces.*

---

## The presenting question

Should the Outreach & Engagement Coordinator be brought into the weekly leadership meeting? On the surface this looks like a meeting design question. As we worked through it, several distinct concerns surfaced — each pointing to a different underlying issue.

---

## What's actually going on (multiple dynamics in one room)

**1. Role clarity for the Coordinator.**
Months of trying to clarify her role; the new JD, worksheet, and strategic doc should help. Open question: should she present the strategic plan + her work to the broader team quarterly?
- *Worry surfaced:* "If she does it, is it taking away my power?"
- *Reframe:* No — leaders who hand others the floor and let them shine are showing more power, not less. The framing matters: "I've asked her to walk us through where we are" positions Tamra as the one who enabled the platform. Trust the instinct to support her shine.

**2. The threatened supervisor.**
One supervisor wants more responsibility and feels threatened when the Coordinator is in the mix. She knows the Coordinator's feelings have been hurt about not being included.

**3. The hurt Coordinator.**
Her work IS leadership-level (just non-supervisory). The implicit message of exclusion from the "leadership meeting" lands as a status statement. She does not currently know about the supervisor's threat dynamic.

**4. Amplifier ask friction.**
The threatened supervisor (and possibly others) may not want to be amplifiers when asked by someone who isn't their boss. Person-to-person asks land as "favor request from a non-boss."
- *Structural fix:* Asks should come from the *strategic plan*, not the Coordinator personally. Tamra (as Director) sets the expectation that supervisors are amplifiers as part of program leadership. Coordinator surfaces *what* needs amplifying; the obligation comes from the role, not the relationship.

**5. Hybrid team challenges + Tamra prep.**
Easy to have side conversations and lose people. Tamra coming to meetings unprepared. Both point to the same fix: a standing structured agenda (e.g., 4 fixed sections every week) that doesn't require fresh prep — Tamra just runs the structure.

**6. Felt safety — the most significant constraint.**
The Coordinator is a FASD caregiver and is friendly with people the threatened supervisor supervises. The leadership meeting currently includes conversations about the problems of supervising those very people.
- This is a structural confidentiality issue, not a soft concern.
- The supervisor needs space to talk honestly about supervision.
- The Coordinator would be in an impossible position hearing things about friends.
- *Lived experience is a real asset for this work — and it comes with real conflict-of-interest situations that have to be structured around, not pretended away.*

---

## How to name the exclusion with the Coordinator

If/when this conversation happens with her, the honest framing is structural, not status:

> "There are personnel and supervision conversations that have to happen in a small confidential circle. Because you're personally close to some of the people we discuss, being in that room would put you in a position that isn't fair to you, the staff, or your supervisor. That's not about your role or your value — it's about protecting all of you."

That's true, respectful, and depersonalizes the issue.

---

## The deeper issue: supervision design, not meeting design

The leadership meeting became a catch-all because there's no other place where shared-program decisions can live. The supervision lines themselves are unclear — supervision is split between **location** (Sarasota / Orlando) and **program type** (virtual intake, in-person intake, MH intern program, clinic, etc.). Both supervisors end up overseeing pieces of the same programs.

When supervision lines are unclear, three things happen automatically:
1. Personnel issues that touch both supervisors' domains *have* to be coordinated in a group setting
2. Authority feels ambiguous to supervisors and staff
3. Sensitivity to perceived encroachment goes up — which fuels the threat dynamic

**Restructuring only the meeting won't solve it.** The meeting will keep getting pulled toward whatever isn't otherwise resolved.

---

## Three common models for resolving location-vs-program supervision

None is objectively right — they're tradeoffs.

1. **Primary supervisor for people, program lead for work direction.** Each staff member has *one* HR/people supervisor (reviews, time off, performance, relationship). They may also have a program lead who directs *what* they work on. Clear rule: people questions → people supervisor; work questions → program lead.

2. **Location-based supervision, program-based coordination.** Each supervisor owns the people at her site, period. Programs that span both sites have a designated "program owner" — but that's coordination, not supervision.

3. **Program-based supervision, location-based collaboration.** Supervisor follows the program. Sites coordinate locally for logistics.

The right answer depends on what consistency matters most — *people experience* (usually location wins) or *service experience* (usually program wins).

## A possible meeting shape (to react to)

- **Weekly Supervisor Sync** — supervisors + Tamra. Operational/people-management. Standing agenda. Coordinator NOT at this table. The threatened supervisor keeps her seat and her role here is clearer.
- **Monthly Program Leadership Meeting** — supervisors + Coordinator + Tamra. Strategic and cross-program work. Explicitly scoped to *no individual personnel content* so it stays a place the Coordinator can be in. Calling THIS the leadership meeting positions her as program leadership (which she is).
- **Quarterly all-team strategic update** — Coordinator presents strategic plan progress at the existing weekly team meeting. Tamra opens it. Solves the team's "what does she do?" question without weekly performance pressure.

---

## Practical next steps that don't require redesigning everything immediately

1. **Name a "primary supervisor" for each staff member** for people-stuff (reviews, time off, performance). Even if program oversight is shared, having one clear person for the human side reduces ambiguity and gives each supervisor a defined territory she can feel secure in.
2. **List the decisions/issues that have ended up in the leadership meeting over the last 3–6 months.** Sort them: "Could this be owned by one person if we made a clear rule?" vs. "This genuinely needs both supervisors aligned." The first category points to where to simplify; the second tells you what the meeting is actually for.
3. **Have the 1:1 conversations** with the threatened supervisor and with the Coordinator before changing meeting structure. The structure changes will get filtered through unresolved tension if the underlying dynamics aren't named first.

## Questions still sitting open

- When you say it's been hard to figure out who supervises who — is that something you've actively been trying to resolve, or has the structure grown organically without bandwidth to address it? If the latter, this is the actual lever — bigger than the meeting question, but the one that fixes more.
- Do staff currently know who their primary supervisor is?
- When there's a conflict (e.g., approving time off, performance review), who decides today?
- What do the supervisors want from a touchpoint with the Coordinator? Have they been asked?
- What would you most regret losing if you restructured the meeting? (That answer probably tells you what to protect.)
